How often do you think about space junk?

NASA's SSC tracks sattelites. That represents less than five percent of the around 11 000 Payloads currently orbiting the earth (Spacetrack, 2023)

Space junk is a building issue for future generations, one that I am using this chance to bring attention to both because it's worth talking about, and also because I believe we need to readjust the way we, as a society, discuss future problems.

This graph shows how many satellites NASA's SSC has started tracking on its database by half decade. It gives a good idea of the quantity of launches from the world's leading space agency, which can then be extrapolated to the rest of the world. In particular, it's plain to see there has been a near constant increase in the number of launches from 1950 when the first launched happened to now.

The problem isn't just the amount of sattelites and payloads. No, the real problem is the vast amounts of debris that results from the launch of these sattelites. Everything from massive boosters to broken parts to even loose screws can be a problem.

More satellites mean more junk, and more junk also means more destroyed satellites, perpetuating the cycle even more. Yes, space junk is already causing havoc, even if it isn't doing damage to the earth yet. Satellites are in constant danger of being destroyed, and the International Space Station in particular is constantly having to dodge space debris, which represents a large and genuine threat to all those living aboard the station.

This isn't just a NASA problem either. While they make up most of the launches, other countries like China, France and Japan are also launching sattelites as well. In fact, 2022 was an all time record year for the number of sattelite launches. This can be seen on the graph by how the amount of sattelites launched between 2020 and 2023 is already around half of what was launched during the five years before 2020.

This graph shows the launch and shutdown date of satellites. Active satellites are shown in blue, and inactive ones are shown in black. You can also hover your mouse over the satellites to see their names and status. As you can see from this graph, the majority of satellites are not active, which also reflects the data from space track.

This graph shows the number of active satellites compared to the number of inactive ones.

Be it through bad design or sheer misfortune, satellites are being taken offline and replaced constantly, which this graph shows is becoming increasingly problematic as the number of inactive satellites overtakes the number of working ones.

As you can see from this graph, the majority of satellites last less than twenty years. This means they constantly require replacement, while the inactive satellites contribute to space junk.

So what's the solution to all this? What's the endgame? For now, we don't know. Satellite recovery and disposal is still extremely expensive, not to mention an easily overlooked issue since it doesn't really effect anything directly yet. But that's the keyword, yet. This is a brewing issue for humanity, no two ways about it, and that's why I chose to focus on it for this project. Because by keeping problems in mind, it's easier to avoid exacerbating them, and also it means that when it becomes everyones problem, the discussion has already been going for years.
